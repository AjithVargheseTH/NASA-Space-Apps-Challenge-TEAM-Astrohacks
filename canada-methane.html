Methane Emissions in Canada: Trends, Causes, and Effects

Visual Trends:

  • The bar graph shows a general increase in methane emissions from 1990 to around 1998, peaking in 1997.
  • After 1998, there is a slight decline and stabilization in emissions, with some fluctuations until 2018.

Reasons for Increasing Emissions:

  • Oil and Gas Industry: Around 40% of Canada's methane emissions come from this sector, due to both intentional releases and leaks.
  • Agriculture: Livestock digestion and manure management contribute significantly to methane emissions.
  • Landfills: Decomposition of organic waste in landfills produces methane.
  • Coal Mining: Methane is released during coal mining operations.
  • Wastewater: Treatment of wastewater also generates methane.

After Effects:

  • Climate Change: Methane is a potent greenhouse gas, with at least 28 times the warming potential of CO2 over a 100-year period. It contributes significantly to global warming.
  • Air Quality: Methane emissions can lead to the formation of ground-level ozone, affecting air quality and human health.
  • Ecosystem Impact: Changes in climate can disrupt ecosystems and biodiversity.
  • Economic Costs: Addressing the impacts of climate change, such as infrastructure damage and health costs, can be financially burdensome.
  • Public Health: Methane and associated pollutants can cause lung problems, asthma attacks, and early death from respiratory and cardiovascular complications.
