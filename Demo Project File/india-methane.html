Methane Emissions in India: Trends, Causes, and Effects

Visual Trends:

  • The bar graph shows a consistent increase in methane emissions from 1990 to 2018.
  • Emissions start at a lower level and increase significantly, reflecting the country's rapid economic and industrial growth.

Reasons for Increasing Emissions:

  • Agricultural Activities: Methane is a significant byproduct of livestock digestion (enteric fermentation) and manure management. India has a large agricultural sector, particularly livestock farming, which contributes significantly to methane emissions.
  • Energy Production: The extraction and processing of fossil fuels, including coal mining and natural gas production, contribute to methane emissions.
  • Waste Management: Decomposition of organic waste in landfills produces methane. Increased waste generation and inadequate waste management practices have led to higher emissions.
  • Biomass Burning: Burning wood for heating, bushfires, and biofuels also release methane.
  • Rice Farming: Methane is produced during the cultivation of rice in flooded fields.

After Effects:

  • Climate Change: Methane is a potent greenhouse gas, with a global warming potential much higher than carbon dioxide over a 20-year period. Increased methane emissions contribute significantly to global warming.
  • Air Quality: Methane emissions can lead to the formation of ground-level ozone, which is harmful to human health and the environment.
  • Environmental Impact: Changes in climate can disrupt ecosystems and biodiversity.
  • Economic Costs: The financial burden of mitigating climate change impacts, such as infrastructure damage and health costs, can be significant.
  • Public Health: Methane and associated pollutants can cause lung problems, asthma attacks, and early death from respiratory and cardiovascular complications.
